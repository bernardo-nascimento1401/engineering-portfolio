01 · RESEARCH QUESTION

Modeling a Cessna 172 on Titan.

NASA's Dragonfly mission made me curious about fixed-wing aircraft on Titan. I used a Cessna 172 as a reference and modeled lift, drag, propeller thrust, Reynolds number, takeoff performance, payload, and range under Titan's gravity and atmospheric conditions.

What I modeled

  • Lift and minimum flight speed under Titan gravity and atmospheric density.
  • Propeller thrust and aircraft drag at steady flight conditions.
  • Propeller Reynolds-number behavior in Titan’s dense, low-viscosity atmosphere.
  • Aircraft acceleration as a nonlinear differential equation.
  • Velocity as a function of time and range as its definite integral.
  • Propeller-radius changes intended to improve the predicted operating envelope.
02 · MATHEMATICAL MODEL

Flight and range model.

I started with the four forces of flight and used published Cessna specifications to estimate lift and drag parameters. I combined a propeller-thrust relation with aircraft drag, then divided the net force by total mass to obtain acceleration as a function of velocity.

I then wrote acceleration as dv/dt and solved the resulting separable nonlinear differential equation for velocity over time. Integrating that velocity over the estimated fuel duration produced a range estimate for different payloads.

03 · MODEL RESULTS

Predicted performance.

Under the assumptions I chose, Titan's low gravity and dense atmosphere produced a low stall speed and a large theoretical payload. The propeller Reynolds-number constraint then limited the modeled forward speed to about 11.58 m/s.

11.58m/s baseline modeled max speed
371km modeled full-cargo range
5.64km modeled takeoff distance

The results were mixed. Lift and payload improved, but the low acceleration and propeller limit produced a narrow speed range and a modeled takeoff run of about 5.64 km.

04 · PROPELLER REDESIGN

Propeller redesign.

I made blade radius a design variable and linked it to chord length and blade area. In the model, a 0.373 m blade radius allowed a 13.5 m/s target speed, a 16.6% increase over the baseline case.

0.373m proposed blade radius
13.5m/s redesigned target speed
16.6%Modeled speed increase
This was a modeling exercise, not a flight-ready design. The purpose was to identify the limiting assumptions and use the equations to test one possible geometry change.
