01 · THE CHALLENGE

Accelerate the exhaust without choking the flow—or ignoring integration.

The engine required a compact, subsonic converging nozzle capable of accelerating the exhaust toward a target exit Mach number of 0.9. The design had to satisfy the thermodynamic model while fitting the engine package, connecting to the existing structure, and remaining practical to manufacture.

My contribution

  • Used isentropic compressible-flow relationships to size the nozzle exit area and estimate exit conditions.
  • Developed a converging CAD geometry around engine diameter, packaging, and mounting constraints.
  • Added an integration flange and explored chevron geometry as a potential noise-reduction concept.
  • Used CFD to evaluate flow acceleration, velocity distribution, separation, and downstream streamlines.
  • Compared simulated exit velocity with the analytical prediction to validate the design workflow.
  • Evaluated the Joule–Thomson behavior of hydrogen across a staged high-pressure regulation system.

02 · ANALYTICAL SIZING

Using compressible flow to turn performance targets into geometry.

I used isentropic nozzle relationships to connect stagnation conditions, exit Mach number, temperature, pressure, density, velocity, and mass flow. The calculations determined the exit area required to maintain the target operating point and provided reference values for later simulation.

Rather than treating the analytical model as a one-time calculation, I varied uncertain inputs to understand sensitivity and identify which dimensions remained stable across the expected operating range.

03 · CAD & INTEGRATION

A flowpath designed as part of an engine—not in isolation.

I translated the analytical dimensions into an axisymmetric converging nozzle, then incorporated the interfaces required for engine integration. The model included a mounting flange and attachment pattern, while the external geometry reflected packaging limits around the engine assembly.

I also explored chevrons as a potential exhaust-noise reduction feature. Their role on this prototype was conceptual; acoustic benefit would require dedicated simulation or testing beyond the aerodynamic CFD shown here.

04 · CFD VALIDATION

Checking whether the flow behaved the way the equations predicted.

I used CFD to examine acceleration through the nozzle, compare the outlet velocity to the analytical target, and look for unwanted behavior such as separation, recirculation, or local flow nonuniformity. Velocity contours provided a direct view of how the converging area accelerated the flow.

Downstream streamline visualization helped evaluate the exhaust pattern after it left the nozzle and revealed how the chevron geometry influenced the local wake structure.

05 · HYDROGEN PRESSURE REGULATION

Following the thermodynamics beyond the engine core.

The fuel system reduces hydrogen from roughly 200 atm storage pressure to an approximately 20 atm engine supply through staged regulation. I investigated the Joule–Thomson effect to understand whether that pressure reduction could introduce damaging temperature changes or freeze-out risk in regulators and tubing.

Hydrogen behaves differently from many common gases near room temperature: under relevant conditions, throttling can produce warming rather than cooling. I used published inversion-curve data to assess the sign and expected magnitude of the effect and to inform the pressure-reduction strategy.

06 · OUTCOME

A design workflow linking thermodynamics, geometry, simulation, and system safety.

The project gave me experience carrying a propulsion component from first-principles equations through detailed geometry and CFD validation, while also considering how the nozzle interacts with fuel delivery, mounting, manufacturing, and engine-level performance.

The most valuable part of this work was not any single model. It was building agreement between analytical predictions, CAD geometry, numerical simulation, and the constraints of the complete hydrogen engine system.
