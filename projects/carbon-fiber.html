01 · THE CHALLENGE

Turn complex 3D geometry into a manufacturable robotic process.

Carbon-fiber prepreg must conform to complex mold geometry while avoiding wrinkles, gaps, poor contact, and inefficient robot travel. Automating the process required more than a single trajectory: the system had to understand the mold, divide it into meaningful regions, determine a manufacturing order, select suitable tool motions, and coordinate execution through ROS.

My contribution

  • Developed mesh-processing algorithms to segment complex mold geometry into manufacturable regions.
  • Represented regions as a weighted graph for sequence planning and manufacturing-aware traversal.
  • Trained a pairwise ranking model to predict starting regions and subsequent layup order.
  • Developed tool-selection and path-generation logic for flats, edges, corners, holes, and curved regions.
  • Built ROS nodes, services, and interfaces connecting perception, planning, and robotic execution.
  • Created adaptive control logic and visualization for an eight-actuator mold-support system.

02 · SURFACE UNDERSTANDING

Extracting manufacturing structure from triangulated geometry.

I developed a geometry-processing workflow that worked directly with triangulated meshes. Triangle centers and normals supported local surface analysis, while clustering and boundary detection separated broad flats, bends, edges, and other regions with distinct manufacturing requirements.

Principal-component analysis projected local geometry into a stable 2D frame for extracting region dimensions, dominant directions, and path orientation. The result was a representation that preserved both shape and manufacturing context.

03 · SEQUENCE PLANNING

Learning which region should come next.

Each surface region became a graph node. Candidate transitions were described using geometric and manufacturing features including region area, neighborhood size, flatness, travel distance, shared-boundary length, distance from the start, and penalties associated with leaving nearby regions unfinished.

I trained a pairwise neural-ranking model to compare candidate regions rather than predict a full sequence in one step. This allowed the planner to score feasible alternatives at each decision point while incorporating graph structure and manufacturing constraints.

04 · TOOL-PATH GENERATION

Different surfaces require different physical interactions.

The planner mapped geometric region types to suitable tooling and path strategies. Broad flat areas received multiple parallel strokes sized to the available width, while edges and corners used focused single-pass paths aligned with the dominant local axis. Small or constrained features received specialized tools and contact strategies.

The goal was not simply to find the shortest route. The path had to respect adjacency, tool width, surface shape, robot accessibility, and the physical behavior of carbon-fiber prepreg.
05 · SYSTEM INTEGRATION

Connecting perception, planning, control, and hardware through ROS.

I structured the workflow as an integrated set of ROS nodes, topics, services, and actions. Perception data entered the pipeline as a point cloud and mesh; processing services returned segmented geometry; planning components generated region order and tool paths; and the robot interface converted those outputs into executable motion.

I also developed an adaptive mold-support concept using eight linear actuators. Each actuator responded to the roller’s position through a smooth distance-based weighting function, supporting the mold locally while preserving continuous motion. RViz visualization made actuator state, roller position, and mold alignment visible during development.

06 · RESEARCH COMMUNICATION

The complete IntelliDrape research poster.

I presented the mesh-graph representation, pairwise neural-ranking approach, training objectives, results, limitations, and future work in a single technical poster. The poster connects the algorithmic decisions to the larger goal of automating a labor-intensive composite-manufacturing process.

07 · OUTCOME

A unified workflow across software and physical manufacturing.

The project combined geometry processing, machine learning, graph algorithms, ROS architecture, robotic motion, and adaptive hardware into a single manufacturing pipeline. It taught me how to design algorithms around physical process constraints—and how to make individually strong components operate coherently as a system.
