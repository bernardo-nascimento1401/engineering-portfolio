Micro-UAV Optical Sensor PCB

At Penn's ModLab, I designed a compact photodiode circuit and 7 × 7 mm PCB for lighthouse-based localization on a mini UAV.

01 · THE CHALLENGE

Photodiode sensing for a 7 × 7 mm board.

The mini UAV uses infrared sweeps from a lighthouse tracking system. The sensor board has to convert the photodiode current into clear timing pulses for the flight computer while fitting inside a very small airframe and operating from a 3.3 V supply.

My contribution

  • Designed a photodiode analog front end around a compact dual op-amp.
  • Combined a transimpedance stage, filtering, and a second gain stage.
  • Built a breadboard prototype to verify signal behavior before committing to a PCB.
  • Used an oscilloscope to confirm clean, repeatable pulses from the lighthouse sweep.
  • Selected small packages and made custom KiCad footprints for parts that did not fit the available board area.
  • Laid out a two-layer 7 × 7 mm board in KiCad with power, signal, and connector routing.

02 · ANALOG FRONT END

Analog front-end design.

The first stage converts the photodiode current into a voltage with a transimpedance amplifier. The second stage adds gain and filtering so the lighthouse sweep produces a pulse that the downstream timing electronics can detect reliably.

I selected the TLV9062 dual op-amp so both stages could share one compact package while still meeting the bandwidth and rail-to-rail requirements of the 3.3 V system.

03 · BENCH VALIDATION

Breadboard and oscilloscope testing.

Before laying out the PCB, I built the circuit on a breadboard and tested it with the lighthouse source. The oscilloscope showed distinct, repeatable pulses, which confirmed that the topology and component values were suitable for the next design step.

04 · PCB MINIATURIZATION

7 × 7 mm PCB layout.

The 49 mm² board left little room for routing. I placed the dual op-amp near the center, kept signal paths short, preserved usable ground and power connections, and fit the connector pads along the board edge. I also created custom footprints where the standard options were too large.

05 · OUTCOME

Final design.

The finished design includes the photodiode input, two-stage analog conditioning, 3.3 V power connections, and the output interface on a 7 × 7 mm two-layer board. The project gave me hands-on experience with analog design, bench testing, footprint creation, and layout under a strict size limit.

The breadboard proved the circuit topology before I committed it to the compact PCB layout.
