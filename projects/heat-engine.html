01 · DESIGN

A moving assembly designed for both function and manufacture.

I modeled the full engine in SolidWorks using mates that reproduced the intended motion of the crank, flywheel, connecting linkages, displacer, and power piston. I gave the base and flywheel an aerospace-inspired visual language while remaining within the dimensional and interface constraints of the course design.

Skills developed

  • Parametric part and assembly modeling in SolidWorks.
  • Functional mates and interference-aware assembly design.
  • Precision turning, milling, drilling, boring, and hole-making.
  • Tool setup, workholding, feeds, speeds, and chip-control fundamentals.
  • Calipers, micrometers, dial indicators, and inspection technique.
  • Engineering drawings, tolerances, limits and fits, and GD&T.

02 · DRAWINGS & TOLERANCES

Translate design intent into dimensions a machinist can trust.

Each component began with an engineering drawing that defined material, critical diameters, shoulder locations, threads, fits, and geometric requirements. Reading and producing these drawings reinforced how tolerances control assembly behavior—and how overly tight tolerances increase manufacturing time without necessarily improving function.

03 · MACHINING

Plan the setup, choose the tool, and control the cut.

I manufactured components through a sequence of lathe, mill, drilling, and CNC operations. The work required selecting datums, minimizing unnecessary re-clamping, protecting finished surfaces, and measuring frequently enough to catch variation before a part moved out of tolerance.

04 · ASSEMBLY

Small dimensional errors become system-level friction.

Final assembly tied the individual operations together. Shafts, bushings, links, flywheel, frame, and thermal components had to align with low enough friction to allow the engine to start and sustain motion from heat input alone. The CAD model helped anticipate interfaces, but hand fitting and careful inspection remained essential.

05 · RESULT

A functional engine that reached 1,700 RPM under heat alone.

After manufacturing and assembling the full mechanism, the engine sustained rotation and reached approximately 1,700 RPM. The result validated not only the thermodynamic concept but also the accumulated quality of dozens of machining, tolerance, alignment, and assembly decisions.

This project made manufacturing constraints tangible: a design is only successful when its dimensions, tolerances, finishes, and interfaces can be produced and assembled into a system that actually runs.
